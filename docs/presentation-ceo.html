CEO Briefing · 30 min

What We're Building

A documented knowledge architecture across four connected dimensions — plus a structure for helping materials like wiki pages, manuals, and templates.

Actions

What the organization does — processes along the value chain, who is responsible, which data and applications are involved.

Applications

The systems that support the work — the software portfolio, strategically classified and linked to actions and domains.

Knowledge Domains & Entities

What the organization knows — business data organized into bounded domains, each with a named owner accountable for quality.

Integrations

How data flows between systems — which applications exchange data, how mature and reliable each flow is.

These form a connected graph — a change in one dimension can be traced across all others.

The Documentation Tool

A browser-based visualization tool serves as the single source of truth.

Populated step by step

After each phase, results are migrated into the tool. The architecture grows incrementally.

Always current

Interactive views show the architecture as it stands — service blueprint, application landscape, entity map, integrations.

Living documentation

At the end of the process, the tool becomes the organization's ongoing reference for its knowledge architecture.

Easy to navigate

Interactive and browsable — click through processes, applications, domains, and integrations to explore how everything connects.

Key Roles

Knowledge Architect (KA)

External specialist. Designs the data model, runs workshops, migrates results into the tool. Leaves after Phase 8 — mission complete.

Knowledge Facilitator (KF)

Internal coordinator. Manages scheduling, communication, and tracks progress. Stays on permanently to drive implementation.

Knowledge Owners (KO)

One per knowledge domain. Accountable for data quality and governance. Research and document their domain's business entities.

Application Owners (AO)

One per application. Accountable for configuration, integrations, and strategic classification. Document their application's data flows.

Support Layer: Management

Top Management sponsors the initiative and sets strategic direction. Middle Management participates in workshops and ensures their teams engage with the process.

What You Get

Documented As-Is Architecture

Service blueprint, business entities with attribute-level governance, application landscape, and all integrations — visible and interactive in one tool.

Risk & Quality Visibility

Data quality issues, fragile integrations, and governance gaps are surfaced and assessed — no more hidden single points of failure.

To-Be Architecture

A target knowledge architecture, reviewed and agreed upon by the organization, with a concrete plan for how to get there.

Organizational Capability

Named owners for every domain and application. A facilitator to drive ongoing optimization. The process continues without external dependency.

Beyond operational data, the organization has manuals, how-tos, templates, and policies. How should they fit into the architecture?

Option A: Separate

  • Helping documents stay where they are (Confluence, file shares, email)
  • The knowledge architecture focuses only on operational data: entities, applications, integrations
  • Documents are managed informally by each team

Pro: Simple, no extra effort — the architecture stays focused on its core purpose

Con: No visibility into what guides exist, who owns them, or whether they're current

Option B: Integrated

  • Every helping document is cataloged as a "Knowledge Resource" in the architecture
  • Each resource links to the actions it supports and the knowledge domain that owns it
  • The service blueprint shows not just what data an action uses, but what guides help perform it

Pro: Complete picture — you can see for any process step what support exists

Con: Requires cataloging effort and ongoing maintenance

The right answer is likely in between. The question is: how much visibility into helping knowledge does the organization need?
